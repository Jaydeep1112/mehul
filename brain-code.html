What "Brain & Code" Means to Me

Thinker | Builder | Reflective Learner

Brain & Code is not a showcase of algorithms. It is a record of how my thinking has evolved while building them. I am interested in intelligence not as a technical abstraction, but as something that interacts with real people, real constraints, and real consequences. This page brings together the work that has shaped that view across healthcare, agriculture, and applied research, and the lessons that emerged when performance alone stopped being enough.

How I Think About Applied Intelligence

Applied Researcher | Systems Thinker

Early in my work with machine learning, I learned how easy it is to optimize for metrics and how misleading that optimization can be outside controlled settings. Accuracy, precision, and recall matter, but they do not capture uncertainty, context, or responsibility.

I now approach applied AI with three guiding principles:
  • Models should assist human judgment, not replace it.
  • Performance must be interpreted in the environment where the system will be used.
  • strong>The cost of being wrong matters as much as the probability of being right.

Everything that follows is built through this lens.

Applied AI in Healthcare

Project Founder | Clinical Collaborator | Responsible Researcher

Origin: Why Pneumonia Was Not an Abstract Problem

My interest in pneumonia detection was not purely academic. During my early teens, I was hospitalized with pneumonia, an experience that exposed me to the uncertainty behind clinical decisions and the consequences of delayed or incorrect diagnosis. Years later, when I encountered medical imaging datasets through AI coursework, pneumonia was no longer just a label. It represented the gap between prediction and care, and the responsibility embedded in that gap.

Pneumonia Detection Model

In Grade 11, I began developing a convolutional neural network to classify chest X-ray images as normal or pneumonia-positive. Working with publicly available datasets, I focused on dataset curation, preprocessing decisions, and validation strategies that balanced performance with generalizability.

The model achieved approximately 93% accuracy. From the outset, this number was treated as a starting point for analysis, not a conclusion.

Clinical Mentorship & Translational Responsibility

As the work progressed, I recognized that further development without clinical grounding would be irresponsible. I sought mentorship from Dr. Lalit Verma, MD, Senior Consultant Physician at Nanavati Max Super Speciality Hospital, Mumbai, who became my clinical mentor and project collaborator over the following two years.

Under his guidance, the project shifted from technical optimization to translational reasoning. Our discussions focused on diagnostic uncertainty, trade-offs between sensitivity and specificity, and the real clinical consequences of false positives and false negatives.

When technical assumptions conflicted with clinical realities, I was expected to reassess, retrain, and recalibrate the model, even when doing so reduced apparent performance. Accuracy was never treated as an endpoint. Patient safety and interpretability were treated as constraints.

Before concluding the work, I prepared a detailed technical and ethical report synthesizing model behavior, limitations, and clinical implications for Dr. Verma’s review.

Applied AI in Agriculture

Team Lead | Mentor | Field Researcher

Wheat Rust Detection

In parallel with healthcare work, I led the development of a wheat rust detection model aimed at early identification of crop disease. While technically similar in architecture, this project introduced a different set of constraints, including inconsistent image quality, environmental variability, and limited computational resources.

The model was trained on field-sourced images with deployability prioritized over laboratory benchmarks.

Learning From the Field

Working with farmers and understanding real usage conditions forced design decisions that favored robustness over elegance. The project reinforced a recurring lesson: a technically correct system can still be practically wrong.

Across healthcare and agriculture, the same principle held. Performance without context is fragile.

From Models to Meaning

Ethical Technologist | Critical Thinker

Looking across these projects, a pattern became clear:

  • Technical competence creates possibility.
  • Judgment determines impact.

Mentorship revealed blind spots. Field research revealed constraints. Reflection revealed responsibility. I no longer view AI as a pursuit of optimal answers, but as a discipline of managing uncertainty with care.

What I'm Carrying Forward

Student Researcher | Long-Term Builder

These experiences now anchor my academic direction and research mindset. I intend to continue working at the intersection of computation and real-world systems, guided by humility, collaboration, and accountability.

What matters to me is not building the most impressive system, but building the most trustworthy one.

Closing Reflection

Learner First | Builder Always

Brain & Code reflects how I learn: by building, questioning, recalibrating, and learning again. It is less about what I have completed and more about the standards I now hold myself to, standards shaped by responsibility, mentorship, and lived consequence.
