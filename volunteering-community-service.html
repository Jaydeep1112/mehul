Volunteering & Community Service

Cleanup Drives | Restoration Projects | Old Age Homes

Since starting my educational path, I've never thought that learning stopped inside the classroom. Volunteering for several community projects has provided me with the opportunity to see it firsthand. From collecting trash, reclaiming public places, or just hanging out with the elderly, these experiences taught me not only as a student but also as a more empathetic and centred person.

Cleanup & Restoration Drives

I actively participated in a number of neighbourhood cleanup drives, where I collaborated with fellow students to contribute to cleaning up parks, school grounds, and abandoned public areas. I learned the importance of taking care of the environment and how repeated small actions can cumulatively make a difference at a bigger level.

Being a part of restoration activities such as repainting walls, planting foliage, and sanitizing heritage sites gave me the opportunity to see the transformation of the forgotten spaces within the community into sites that people could again be proud to own.

There was great satisfaction in physically contributing toward something that made life a little bit better, even in small ways, for the people around us.

Koshish Old Age Visit 2024

Old Age Home Visits

In 2024, I had the opportunity to volunteer at an old age home through the Koshish Foundation. At the time, I believed the purpose of the visit was simple: bring essential supplies, spend time with the residents, and hopefully make their day a little brighter.

Along with blankets and daily necessities, we brought a Saregama Caravaan speaker filled with classic Hindi songs from earlier decades. The moment the music began to play, the atmosphere slowly transformed. Residents who had been sitting quietly began humming along. Some clapped gently. Others closed their eyes and smiled, as if the music had taken them back to a different chapter of their lives.

It was remarkable to see how music could awaken memories so vividly.

One elderly man, his voice soft and emotional, said to me, “Beta, today I felt youthful again.” Another resident held my hand and quietly shared, “We never get visitors here… but today, you gave us family.”

Those words stayed with me long after the visit ended. I had arrived thinking that volunteering meant giving — giving supplies, giving time, giving joy. But as conversations unfolded throughout the afternoon, I slowly began to understand something deeper.

What many residents valued most was not what we had brought with us, but the simple presence of someone willing to sit beside them and listen.

Loneliness in old age often does not come from a lack of resources. It comes from a lack of human connection. A conversation that lasted only a few minutes seemed to mean far more than the donations we carried with us.

The afternoon eventually filled with music, shared meals, games, and laughter. Yet the most meaningful realization for me was how easily elderly individuals can become invisible in a fast-moving society.

For many of the residents, the greatest comfort that day was simply knowing that someone had come to spend time with them. That experience reshaped the way I began to think about service. True service is not always about solving a problem immediately. Sometimes it begins with something far simpler: recognizing another person’s dignity and reminding them that they are not forgotten.

Koshish Old Age Visit 2025

Elder Care Visit (2025)

Returning with Responsibility

In 2025, I returned to the same old age home through the Koshish Foundation. This time the experience began days before the visit itself.

Several of us gathered beside a truck unloading cartons of supplies. We sorted donations, organized toiletries, packed kits, and stacked boxes as if we were running a small warehouse. It was not glamorous work. There were no photographs or applause. But it was necessary.

Before you can create moments of joy, you first have to create dignity.

We packed kits with everyday essentials such as toothpaste, soap, and basic toiletries. These are ordinary items in most households, but for many residents they are not always guaranteed.

On the day of the visit, I helped lead the volunteering effort alongside a few close friends. Together we distributed these kits to nearly 120 dadas and dadis.

But the number itself was not what stayed with me.

During the visit, between the music, games, and conversations, I sat beside one grandmother and spoke with her the way I would speak with someone in my own family.

She asked me which grade I was in.
“Twelfth,” I replied.

Before I could continue, she gently placed her hand on my head and said, “You have the wishes of all the dadas and dadis here. You will do very well in your exams.”

Then she paused and added quietly, “When you do, promise us you will come back.”

That moment stayed with me long after the visit ended. It reminded me that loneliness does not always appear as sadness. Sometimes it appears as a smile that is just a little too grateful for simple company.

I am very close to my own grandmother, so perhaps that is why the moment felt especially personal. Sitting in that room, I did not see strangers. I saw people who had raised families, carried responsibilities for decades, and still had enough warmth to bless the future of someone they had just met.

My grandmother once told me something I have carried with me for years: do not count life only in years, count it in the genuine smiles you leave behind.

That day, I began to understand what she meant.

Returning to the old age home showed me that service is not always about doing something extraordinary. Sometimes it is simply about ensuring that someone feels remembered, valued, and included in the world around them.
